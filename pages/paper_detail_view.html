Abstract

This paper examines the nature of syntactic islands across typologically diverse languages, providing new insights into the universal principles governing extraction phenomena within the Minimalist Program framework. Through a comprehensive analysis of wh-movement, topicalization, and focus constructions in twelve languages from different families, we demonstrate that island effects emerge from the interaction between phase-based derivation and interface conditions.

Our investigation reveals that traditional island constraints, while descriptively accurate, require reinterpretation in terms of computational efficiency and interpretive requirements at the syntax-semantics interface. We propose a unified account where apparent island violations in certain languages result from alternative derivational pathways that satisfy both syntactic and semantic well-formedness conditions.

The cross-linguistic data presented here challenges previous assumptions about the universality of specific island types, suggesting instead that variation in extraction possibilities reflects different parameter settings within a constrained universal grammar. These findings have significant implications for our understanding of syntactic computation and the architecture of the language faculty.

Keywords: syntactic islands, Minimalist Program, cross-linguistic variation, wh-movement, phase theory, extraction phenomena, universal grammar, computational efficiency

Research Context

ConSOLE 30 Theme: "Bridging Theory and Data"

This paper was presented at ConSOLE 30, hosted by the University of Leiden in March 2023. The conference theme emphasized the crucial connection between theoretical frameworks and empirical linguistic data, making this cross-linguistic investigation particularly relevant to the academic discourse.

Historical Context

Builds upon foundational work in island theory from Ross (1967) and subsequent developments in Government and Binding theory, updating these insights for the Minimalist Program era.

Methodological Innovation

Employs a novel comparative methodology combining acceptability judgments with corpus analysis across twelve typologically diverse languages.
